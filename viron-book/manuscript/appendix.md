## VIRON Quick Reference Card

### The Five Layers

**Capture** — Notes Inbox — Has this been externalized?

**Thinking** — Notes (Projects, Library) — Why? What? How?

**Execution** — Reminders — What must be done?

**Time** — Calendar — When will this happen?

**Knowledge** — Notes Library — What endures from this work?

### The Folder Structure

```
VIRON/
├── Inbox       — all unprocessed capture
├── Projects    — active project notes
├── Templates   — the four core templates
├── Library     — distilled, durable principles
└── Archive     — completed projects and closed material
```

### The Reminders Lists

- **Inbox** — quick captures waiting for processing
- **VIRON Tasks** — master task list (non-project tasks)
- **[Project Name]** — one list per active project (max five)

### The Four Shortcuts

**Quick Capture** — any moment — task or idea lands in Inbox

**Convert Note to Task** — highlight in Notes — selected text becomes Reminder

**Schedule Task Block** — choose Reminder — Reminder becomes Calendar event

**Daily Planning** — morning — unfinished tasks assigned to today

### The Weekly Review Checklist

- [ ] Projects: outcome valid, next action clear, scope clean
- [ ] Inbox: emptied to zero
- [ ] Reminders: stale tasks deleted, vague tasks rewritten
- [ ] Calendar: deep work blocks created for next week
- [ ] Focus theme: one sentence written

### The Decision Filters

**New project:** Does it align with current focus? Does it require pausing something? Does it compound existing work? (Fail two of three = defer)

**New commitment:** Is the time cost recoverable? Does it serve current outcomes? Is declining costly? (Fail two = decline)

**Inbox item:** Actionable now? Compounding? Worth keeping in three months? (Fail all three = delete)

### The Tag Blueprint

**Operational (5–7):** `#task` `#meeting` `#idea` `#decision` `#review`

**Status (3–5):** `#waiting` `#blocked` `#next` `#someday`

**Thematic (8–15):** derived from your recurring work areas. Examples: `#strategy` `#learning` `#process` `#communication` `#product`

### The Maturity Indicators

1. Inbox clears in under fifteen minutes during review.
2. You can name your three most important projects without consulting Notes.
3. The Library contains notes you have referenced more than once.
4. Your doctrine has been revised at least twice.
5. You have deleted at least one project without completing it.
6. The system feels quiet most of the time.

## Recommended Reading

The books below are referenced in Chapter 1 or inform the philosophy behind VIRON. Each entry includes a brief note on its contribution to the architecture.

**Getting Things Done** — David Allen

The foundational work on trusted external systems and the value of a single capture inbox. VIRON's Inbox doctrine and the principle that the mind is not a storage device trace directly to Allen's work. The Weekly Review concept originated here.

**Building a Second Brain** — Tiago Forte

Introduced the idea of a personal knowledge management system built around externalizing and organizing thinking. The VIRON Library layer is a focused, constrained implementation of the second-brain concept. Forte's PARA method — Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives — influenced the VIRON folder structure.

**Deep Work** — Cal Newport

The argument for protected focus time and the cost of context switching. The VIRON discipline of limiting active projects and scheduling deep work blocks in Calendar reflects Newport's core thesis. The Calendar layer in VIRON is the structural implementation of deep work principles.

**Atomic Habits** — James Clear

The mechanics of habit formation and the role of systems in sustaining behavior. VIRON's emphasis on consistent rhythms over motivation — particularly the Weekly Review as a ritual rather than a procedure — reflects Clear's framework.

**How to Take Smart Notes** — Sonke Ahrens

An accessible treatment of the Zettelkasten method and the value of atomic, linked notes. The VIRON Library note format — short, self-contained, titled as a complete principle — draws from this tradition. The idea that notes should be written for retrieval, not storage, is central.

**The ONE Thing** — Gary Keller and Jay Papasan

The case for radical prioritization and the compounding effect of narrowing focus to a single most important task. VIRON's Narrow stage and the daily practice of selecting three to five tasks reflect this constraint philosophy.

## VIRON Glossary

**Archive** — The folder inside VIRON Notes where completed projects and closed material are stored. Not deleted — retained for reference. Distinct from the Library, which holds enduring principles rather than completed work.

**Capture** — The act of externalizing a thought before evaluating or classifying it. The first and most important behavior in VIRON. Capture happens in the Inbox. Classification happens during review.

**Doctrine (Personal Operating Doctrine)** — A self-authored set of principles, constraints, and decision filters stored as a Library note. Governs project selection, commitment acceptance, and behavior under pressure. Reviewed and updated quarterly.

**Elevation** — The process of refining raw captured material into structured, actionable, or reusable form. The E in the VIRON acronym. Elevation converts accumulation into intelligence.

**Execution Layer** — Apple Reminders. The single trusted location for all tasks that require action. Separate from the thinking layer (Notes) and the time layer (Calendar).

**Focus Theme** — A single sentence written at the end of the Weekly Review that defines the directional priority for the coming week. Not a goal. A filter for attention decisions.

**Inbox** — A folder inside VIRON Notes and a list inside Apple Reminders, both serving as temporary intake buffers for unprocessed captures. Cleared to zero weekly. Not a backlog.

**Layer Contamination** — A failure mode in which tools absorb responsibilities from other layers. Tasks inside Notes. Ideas inside Reminders. Meeting notes inside Calendar. Contamination increases maintenance cost and reduces trust in any single tool.

**Library** — The knowledge compounding layer in VIRON. A folder inside Notes containing refined, durable principles distilled from completed projects and processed ideas. Grows slowly. Consulted frequently at maturity.

**Minimal Viable VIRON** — A stripped-down entry point for beginners or re-entrants: one folder (Notes Inbox), one list (VIRON Tasks), one weekly review. Sufficient to establish the foundational habits before expanding the architecture.

**Narrowing** — The discipline of reducing options to a manageable and executable set. The N in the VIRON acronym. Applied to projects (max five), daily tasks (three to five), and weekly focus.

**Project Note** — A note inside the Projects folder in VIRON Notes. Contains the outcome, scope, milestones, decisions, and context for a single active project. Does not contain tasks. Tasks live in Reminders.

**Single Responsibility Principle** — The architectural rule that each tool in VIRON should perform one role. Notes thinks. Reminders executes. Calendar allocates time. Blurring these roles introduces ambiguity.

**Smart Folder** — A dynamic view in Apple Notes that aggregates notes by tag. Not a storage location. A visibility lens. VIRON uses a small number of Smart Folders to surface cross-project activity without reorganizing structure.

**Status Tag** — A temporary tag applied to a note to indicate current state. Examples: `#waiting`, `#blocked`, `#next`. Reviewed and cleared weekly. Distinct from thematic tags, which are permanent.

**Thematic Tag** — A stable tag applied to Library notes and some project notes to classify subject matter. Enables retrieval across projects by topic. Derived from recurring themes in your actual work. Reviewed annually.

**Thinking Layer** — Apple Notes. The cognitive workspace of VIRON where ideas form, projects are shaped, decisions are recorded, and knowledge is distilled. Expansive and context-rich. Does not contain tasks.

**Time Layer** — Apple Calendar. Manages when. Meetings, time blocks, and deadlines live here. Calendar does not manage tasks. Reminders does not manage meetings.

**Weekly Review** — The governance ritual that runs once per week and covers five sections: Projects, Inbox, Reminders, Calendar Preview, and Focus Theme. The keystone behavior of VIRON. Cannot be replaced by any other component.
